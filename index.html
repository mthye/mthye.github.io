About

I am a PhD student specializing in human cognitive neuroscience within the Psychology Department at the University of Edinburgh.

I am broadly interested in:
(1) How we acquire and flexibility use different kinds of concepts, both concrete and abstract
(2) How these concepts are represented in the brain
(3) How our conceptual understanding of the world impacts our social experiences and interactions
(4) Whether differences exist across neurotypes, in particular in autistic and non-autistic adults

To investigate these topics, I use cognitive, behavioural, and neuroimaging methods, most consistently functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), to study the semantic cognition system and to explore to what extent social knowledge is housed within this system. Where possible and when the research question is well-suited for it, I use naturalistic stimuli, such as narratives and movies, to better approximate cognition as it occurs in day-to-day life.

My interest in neurotype differences is motivated by the neurodiversity paradigm which acknowledges and values the many different ways of being and engaging with the world. The goal of this line of research is twofold: First, to better understand and, ultimately, provide greater support for the cognitive and neural differences that occur within and between neurotypes. Second, to conduct research with and for autistic people that challenges stigmatizing, deficit-focused accounts of autism.
